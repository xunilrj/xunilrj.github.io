  • They get a property right from the Peruvian government and that property right is in the form of a concession. It establishes that it’s theirs, provided you renew agreements and commitments over so many years, but it’s a property right. And they’re going to have it for 60 years, 120 years, or it’s unlimited. This property right then becomes even more “perfected”—that’s the word we use in Spanish—and thus it becomes even more detailed as to how far these rights go. Then that property right—which has been protected by the bilateral international treaty that has been signed between Peru and the United States—gets inscribed and blessed by the United States Overseas Private Investment Corporation (OPIC), which issues guarantees that makes it interesting for everybody to look at that property, because it’s got the backing of OPIC. Then that property right goes to the Multilateral Investment Guarantee program of the World Bank, where again it is ratified by its 187 member countries, who of course view this as a way of guaranteeing investment in developing nations. Then the owner of that title goes to Toronto, Wall Street, or London and says, “I have got a property right that even the Peruvian congress can’t take away.” On that basis, the investor gets $3 billion and brings it back to Peru. Now they’re right next to tribes who have the same amount of land or even more, but are poor. (https://worldpolicy.org/2010/06/07/this-land-is-your-land/)
